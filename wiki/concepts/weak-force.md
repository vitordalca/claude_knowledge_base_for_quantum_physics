## Summary

The weak force (or weak interaction) is one of the four fundamental forces of nature, responsible for radioactive decay, neutrino interactions, and some particle transformations. Despite its name, the weak force is only "weak" at low energies; at very high energies (above ~100 GeV), it becomes comparable in strength to the electromagnetic force. The weak force is mediated by three massive bosons: the W⁺, W⁻, and Z bosons. Uniquely among the fundamental forces, the weak interaction violates parity symmetry—it preferentially acts on left-handed particles and right-handed antiparticles. The weak force is the only mechanism capable of converting quarks from one type (flavor) to another, enabling processes like neutron decay (crucial for nuclear fusion in stars) and the production of antimatter.

## Resumo

A força fraca (ou interação fraca) é uma das quatro forças fundamentais da natureza, responsável pelo decaimento radioativo, interações de neutrinos e algumas transformações de partículas. Apesar de seu nome, a força fraca é apenas "fraca" em baixas energias; em energias muito altas (acima de ~100 GeV), torna-se comparável em força à força eletromagnética. A força fraca é mediada por três bósons massivos: os bósons W⁺, W⁻ e Z. Única entre as forças fundamentais, a interação fraca viola a simetria de paridade—age preferencialmente em partículas canhotas (left-handed) e antipartículas destras (right-handed). A força fraca é o único mecanismo capaz de converter quarks de um tipo (sabor) para outro, possibilitando processos como decaimento de nêutron (crucial para fusão nuclear em estrelas) e a produção de antimatéria.

## Explanation

### Four Fundamental Forces

The weak force is one of nature's basic interactions:

| Force | Carrier | Strength (relative) | Range | Symmetric? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strong | Gluons | 1 | ~10⁻¹⁵ m | Yes |
| Electromagnetic | Photon | 10⁻² | Infinite | Yes |
| **Weak** | **W±, Z** | **10⁻⁶** | ~10⁻¹⁸ m | **No (parity violating)** |
| Gravitational | Graviton (?) | 10⁻⁴⁵ | Infinite | Yes |

At low energies, the weak force appears extremely weak—hence its name. However, this is misleading: the weakness arises because W and Z bosons are massive (~80–91 GeV), suppressing weak interactions at low energies by the factor (E/m)² where E is the interaction energy.

### Mechanism: Flavor Changing

The weak force's defining feature is its ability to change quark flavor—converting one type of quark into another:

**Down → Up transition** (β⁻ decay):
- A down quark emits a W⁻ boson and transforms into an up quark
- The W⁻ decays into an electron and an antineutrino
- Net effect: neutron (udd) → proton (uud) + electron + antineutrino

**Up → Down transition** (β⁺ decay):
- An up quark emits a W⁺ boson and transforms into a down quark
- The W⁺ decays into a positron and a neutrino
- Net effect: proton (uud) → neutron (udd) + positron + neutrino

This flavor-changing property is unique to the weak force—the strong and electromagnetic forces cannot change quark types.

### Parity Violation

The weak force violates parity (mirror symmetry) in a maximal way:

**Helicity preference**:
- **Particles**: Only left-handed particles (spin pointing backward) feel the weak force
- **Antiparticles**: Only right-handed antiparticles (spin pointing forward) feel the weak force
- Right-handed particles and left-handed antiparticles do not interact via the weak force

**Consequence**: The weak interaction distinguishes between left and right—something forbidden by parity symmetry. This asymmetry was discovered experimentally in 1956–1957 by Chien-Shiung Wu (see [[concepts/parity-violation]]).

### Massive Mediators

The weak force is mediated by three massive bosons:

| Boson | Mass | Charge | Decay Modes |
|---|---|---|---|
| W⁺ | 80.38 GeV | +1 | e⁺νₑ, μ⁺νμ, τ⁺ντ, qq̄' |
| W⁻ | 80.38 GeV | −1 | e⁻ν̄ₑ, μ⁻ν̄μ, τ⁻ν̄τ, q̄q' |
| Z⁰ | 91.19 GeV | 0 | e⁺e⁻, μ⁺μ⁻, τ⁺τ⁻, νν̄, qq̄ |

Their large masses suppress weak interactions at low energies but enable flavor-changing processes at high energies.

### Electroweak Unification

At very high temperatures (early universe) or very high energies (particle colliders), the weak force and electromagnetic force become indistinguishable. This is the **electroweak force**, unified by Sheldon Glashow, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg (1960s–1970s).

**Unification**: At energies above ~100 GeV (corresponding to temperatures of 10¹⁵ K):
- W and Z bosons become massless
- Photons and weak bosons merge into a single unified interaction
- Electroweak symmetry is unbroken
- All particles are massless

**Below 100 GeV**: The Higgs field acquires a vacuum expectation value (see [[concepts/vacuum-expectation-value]]), breaking the electroweak symmetry:
- W and Z bosons gain mass via the Higgs mechanism
- Electromagnetic and weak forces become distinct
- Parity violation becomes manifest

## Current State of the Field

### Experimental Precision

- **W and Z mass measurements**: Precision to <0.1 GeV at LEP and Tevatron
- **Weak coupling constant**: Measured via β-decay half-lives and W/Z properties
- **Neutrino interactions**: Precision measurements of neutrino-nucleus scattering
- **Rare decays**: Searches for rare weak processes sensitive to physics beyond the Standard Model

### Open Questions and Mysteries

1. **Matter-antimatter asymmetry**: The weak force (via CP violation) may hold clues, but the measured CP violation is insufficient to explain the cosmic asymmetry

2. **Neutrino masses**: Weak interactions involve neutrinos, yet they appear to have tiny masses—the origin is unknown

3. **Hierarchy problem**: Why is the weak force so much weaker than the strong force? Unification at higher energies suggests a deeper symmetry

4. **Flavor mixing**: Why are weak interactions so complex, with flavor-mixing angles (CKM matrix) that appear arbitrary?

## Open Questions

- **Why does the weak force violate parity?** What deeper principle underlies this asymmetry?
- **Can the weak force be unified with gravity?** Grand unified theories (GUTs) attempt this, but remain speculative
- **What is the nature of the weak force mediators?** Are W/Z bosons fundamental or composite?
- **Why are neutrino masses so small?** Is there a mechanism (like the seesaw mechanism) that explains tiny neutrino mass?
