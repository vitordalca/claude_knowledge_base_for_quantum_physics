## Summary

The Higgs boson is a fundamental scalar particle (spin 0) discovered at CERN's Large Hadron Collider in 2012, with a mass of 125.10 ± 0.11 GeV. In quantum field theory, it is a quantum of oscillation (a ripple) of the Higgs field. The Higgs boson is the physical manifestation of electroweak symmetry breaking and provides direct evidence for the Higgs mechanism—the process by which W/Z bosons and fermions acquire mass. The discovery was momentous because it confirmed a theoretical prediction made half a century earlier and validated the Standard Model framework.

## Resumo

O bóson de Higgs é uma partícula fundamental escalar (spin 0) descoberta no Grande Colisor de Hádrons do CERN em 2012, com uma massa de 125,10 ± 0,11 GeV. Na teoria quântica de campos, é um quantum de oscilação (uma ondulação) do campo de Higgs. O bóson de Higgs é a manifestação física da quebra de simetria eletrofraca e fornece evidência direta para o mecanismo de Higgs—o processo pelo qual bósons W/Z e férmions adquirem massa. A descoberta foi momentosa porque confirmou uma previsão teórica feita meio século antes e validou o framework do Modelo Padrão.

## Explanation

### What Is the Higgs Boson?

**Definition in Quantum Field Theory**

The Higgs boson is not a "particle" in classical sense but rather:
- A quantum excitation of the Higgs field
- The smallest possible vibration of the Higgs field
- When the Higgs field φ(x,t) oscillates around its background value v, these oscillations h(x,t) are quanta = Higgs bosons

**Mathematical Expression**

Write the Higgs field as: φ(x,t) = v + h(x,t)

where:
- v ≈ 246 GeV = vacuum expectation value (background)
- h(x,t) = small fluctuations around v
- Quanta of h are Higgs bosons

**Properties**

| Property | Value | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Mass | 125.10 ± 0.11 GeV | Fixed by potential curvature |
| Spin | 0 | Scalar boson (S=0) |
| Charge | 0 | Electrically neutral |
| Color | Neutral | Not affected by strong force |
| Lifetime | ~10⁻²² s | Decays immediately to other particles |
| Width | 4.07 ± 0.24 MeV | Measure of decay probability |

### Why Is It Special?

**Evidence for Electroweak Symmetry Breaking**

The Higgs boson directly confirms:
1. The Higgs field exists and has non-zero vev
2. Electroweak symmetry is broken (unified force splits into EM + weak)
3. The mechanism by which mass is generated in Standard Model

**Prediction Half Century Earlier**

- **1960s**: Higgs, Englert, Brout, and others predicted Higgs boson theoretically
- **1960s–2011**: Searches at various colliders; no evidence
- **2012**: Discovery at LHC; confirmed theoretical framework

**Precision Test of Standard Model**

Properties measured with high precision:
- Mass measurement constrains Standard Model parameters
- Branching ratios (how it decays) must match SM predictions
- Any deviation from SM would hint at new physics

### The 2012 Discovery

**LHC Experiment Setup**

- **Proton-proton collisions** at 7–8 TeV center-of-mass energy
- **Two detectors**: ATLAS and CMS (independent experiments)
- **Both see same signal** → Credibility (not detector artifact)

**The Signal**

- Higgs too unstable to observe directly
- Observe it via decay products (typically to photon pairs or W/Z pairs)
- Small "bump" in background noise at m ≈ 125 GeV
- Bump significance: 5 sigma (1 in 3.5 million chance of being noise)

**Quoted Discovery**: "This is it. We have discovered a Higgs boson."

### Higgs Boson Decays

**Why It Doesn't Last**

The Higgs boson is unstable; it decays into lighter particles within ~10⁻²² seconds.

**Main Decay Channels** (Standard Model predictions)

| Decay Mode | Branching Ratio | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| H → γγ (two photons) | 0.227% | Clean, golden channel |
| H → ZZ* → 4 leptons | 0.124% | Very clean |
| H → WW* → 2 leptons + 2 neutrinos | 21.5% | Largest branching ratio |
| H → bb̄ (bottom quarks) | 58.2% | Dominant but hard to detect |
| H → ττ (tau pairs) | 6.27% | Clean decay |
| H → gg (gluons) | 8.57% | Background heavy |

**Why Multiple Decay Channels?**

The Higgs couples to all massive particles proportionally to their mass. Heavier particles are preferred targets for decay (higher branching ratio), but sometimes lighter decay products are easier to detect experimentally.

### Mass: Why 125 GeV?

**The Question**

Before discovery, physicists knew Higgs *had to exist* (for theoretical consistency) but couldn't predict its mass. Why?

**The Answer from Higgs Mechanism**

The Higgs mass is determined by the potential curvature at the minimum:
$$m_H = \hbar \sqrt{\frac{d^2V}{d\phi^2}\bigg|_{\phi=v}} / c^2$$

For Mexican hat potential V(φ) = −μ²φ²/2 + λφ⁴/4:
$$m_H = \hbar \sqrt{2\mu^2} / c^2$$

- **v = μ/√λ** was known from precision weak-force measurements
- **λ** (potential steepness) was **NOT known**
- Therefore, **m_H was unpredictable** until direct measurement

The 2012 discovery fixed λ, determining the potential shape.

### Coupling to Particles

**Universal Principle**

Higgs couples to all massive particles with strength proportional to their mass:

$$g_{HXX} \propto m_X$$

This is why:
- Top quark (173 GeV) couples very strongly to Higgs
- Bottom quark (4.2 GeV) couples moderately
- Electron (0.511 MeV) couples very weakly
- Photon (massless) doesn't couple at all

**Consequence**: Higgs branching ratios determined entirely by particle masses.

## Current State of the Field

### Precision Measurements (2012–2026)

**Mass Precision**
- ATLAS + CMS combined: 125.10 ± 0.11 GeV
- Improvement from initial ±0.2 GeV to ±0.1 GeV (factor 2 better)

**Branching Ratios**
- H→γγ: Measured to ±0.1 (SM prediction 0.227%)
- H→WW: Measured to ±0.15 (SM prediction 21.5%)
- H→bb: Measured to ±0.2 (SM prediction 58.2%)
- All consistent with SM to within uncertainties

**Coupling Measurements**
- Individual couplings (g_HWW, g_HZZ, g_Htt, g_Hbb, etc.) all measured
- Scale by mass as expected
- No anomalies detected

### Searches for New Physics

**Exotic Higgs Decays**
- H → dark matter (invisible): Constrained; BR < 10–20%
- H → light pseudoscalars: Searches ongoing
- H → supersymmetric particles: None observed (constrains SUSY scale)

**Invisible Higgs Width**
- If Higgs decays to dark matter, width increases
- Precision measurements constrain this; still possible but limited

**Double Higgs Production**
- Created two Higgs bosons in single collision
- First evidence 2018; still being measured
- Tests Higgs self-coupling (λ parameter)

### Remaining Questions

1. **Why 125 GeV?** Is there a deeper principle fixing λ, or is it accident?
2. **Are there more Higgs bosons?** SUSY, 2HDM, extra-dimensional models predict extras
3. **Higgs self-coupling**: Can we measure λ precisely to test potential shape?
4. **Stability of Higgs potential**: Does electroweak vacuum remain stable forever?
5. **Role in early universe**: How did Higgs field behave at high temperatures (phase transitions)?

## Open Questions

- **Could there be Higgs "cousins"?** Other scalar particles with similar properties?
- **Is the Higgs fundamental or composite?** Current data suggest fundamental, but could be composite at high energy
- **Does Higgs explain all mass?** No; proton/neutron mass mostly from QCD binding energy
- **Can Higgs be used to access dark sector?** Possible if dark sector particles couple to Higgs
- **What about graviton?** Gravity not quantized in SM; graviton (if exists) would be separate
