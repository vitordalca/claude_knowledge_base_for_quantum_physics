## Summary

Parity violation is the phenomenon whereby the weak interaction breaks mirror-reflection symmetry (parity). This groundbreaking discovery in 1956–1957 by Chien-Shiung Wu and colleagues overturned the belief that fundamental physical laws were symmetric under mirror inversion. In parity violation, a physical process and its mirror image produce different outcomes—the weak interaction preferentially acts on particles with specific helicity (spin orientation relative to momentum). This discovery was revolutionary because it revealed that not all fundamental forces preserve parity, fundamentally altering our understanding of symmetry in nature.

## Explanation

### Concept of Parity

Parity (P) is a symmetry operation that mirrors a physical system: **x → −x, y → −y, z → −z** (spatial inversion). For a process to conserve parity, its mirror image must follow identical physical laws.

**Classical examples**:
- A ball thrown with a certain trajectory follows the same laws whether observed in a mirror or from the original perspective
- Electromagnetic forces are parity-conserving: a charged particle accelerates in the same way in a mirrored electric field

**Before 1957**: Physicists believed all fundamental interactions conserved parity. This was a deeply held conviction based on experimental evidence in the strong and electromagnetic interactions.

### Historical Discovery

**The τ-θ puzzle (1950s)**: Two particles, the τ meson and θ meson, had identical mass and lifetime but decayed into products with different parity. They appeared to be the same particle yet violated parity conservation—an apparent contradiction.

**Lee and Yang insight (1956)**: T.D. Lee and C.N. Yang proposed that parity might not be conserved in the weak interaction. They examined the evidence and found that while strong and electromagnetic interactions clearly conserved parity, the weak interaction had never been tested!

**Wu experiment (December 1956)**: Chien-Shiung Wu's team cooled cobalt-60 nuclei to near absolute zero, polarized their spins magnetically, and observed their beta decay:

- **Expected (parity-conserving)**: Electrons should be emitted equally in all directions
- **Observed (parity-violating)**: Electrons preferentially emerged in the direction opposite to the nuclear spin

This asymmetry proved that parity was violated in weak interactions.

### The Weak Force and Helicity

Parity violation in weak interactions is intimately connected to **helicity**—the alignment of a particle's spin with its momentum direction:

- **Right-handed**: Spin points forward (along momentum)
- **Left-handed**: Spin points backward (opposite to momentum)

**The key finding**: In weak interactions, **only left-handed particles (and right-handed antiparticles) participate**. This is an intrinsic asymmetry at the fundamental level.

For example, in beta decay (neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino):
- The emitted electron is predominantly left-handed
- The antineutrino is purely right-handed

This handedness preference is why the weak force is uniquely asymmetric.

### CPT Symmetry and Remaining Symmetries

While parity (P) alone is violated in weak interactions, physicists discovered that **combined symmetries remain respected**:

- **Charge conjugation (C)**: Swapping particles ↔ antiparticles. Violated in weak interactions.
- **Combined CP**: Parity inversion + particle-antiparticle swap. Violated very rarely (in rare kaon and B meson decays)
- **CPT**: Parity inversion + particle-antiparticle swap + time reversal. Appears to be exact (fundamental theorem of relativistic QFT)

## Current State of the Field

### Precision Tests

- **CPT symmetry tests**: Comparing properties of particles and antiparticles to parts per billion (antimatter experiments at CERN)
- **β-decay asymmetry measurements**: Testing the degree of left-handed vs. right-handed electron emission
- **Neutral meson oscillations**: K mesons and B mesons exhibit rare CP violation, deepening understanding of weak interactions

### Implications for Particle Physics

- **Electroweak unification**: Parity violation evidence supported the unified electroweak theory (Glashow, Weinberg, Salam), which unified weak and electromagnetic forces
- **Matter-antimatter asymmetry**: CP violation (related to parity violation) may explain why the universe contains more matter than antimatter
- **Handedness in nature**: The weak force's preference for left-handed particles has profound implications for why biological molecules are predominantly left-handed

## Open Questions

- **Why does the weak force prefer left-handed particles?** This remains one of the deepest unanswered questions in physics
- **Is CPT symmetry truly exact?** Despite extensive tests, proving absolute CPT conservation remains an open frontier
- **What deeper symmetry underlies parity violation?** Future theories may explain why the weak force breaks parity
- **Can CP violation be observed in other interactions?** Current evidence suggests CP violation is rare; understanding its full scope is ongoing
