## Summary

Antihydrogen is the antimatter counterpart of the hydrogen atom: a bound state of one antiproton and one positron. Unlike ordinary hydrogen (proton + electron), antihydrogen has opposite charges but identical mass and quantum properties. Antihydrogen atoms are electrically neutral, making them difficult to trap directly, but they can be confined using magnetic bottles. CERN's ALPHA collaboration has produced and trapped thousands of antihydrogen atoms, enabling precision tests of matter-antimatter symmetry and, most notably, measuring the gravitational acceleration of antimatter itself—a landmark experiment that confirmed antimatter falls downward like ordinary matter.

## Resumo

Anti-hidrogênio é o equivalente de antimatéria do átomo de hidrogênio: um estado ligado de um antiproton e um positron. Ao contrário do hidrogênio ordinário (próton + elétron), o anti-hidrogênio tem cargas opostas mas massa e propriedades quânticas idênticas. Átomos de anti-hidrogênio são eletricamente neutros, dificultando sua captura direta, mas podem ser confinados usando garrafas magnéticas. A colaboração ALPHA do CERN produziu e aprisionou milhares de átomos de anti-hidrogênio, possibilitando testes de precisão da simetria matéria-antimatéria e, mais notavelmente, medindo a aceleração gravitacional da antimatéria em si—um experimento marco que confirmou que a antimatéria cai para baixo como a matéria ordinária.

## Explanation

### Composition and Formation

Antihydrogen consists of:
- **1 antiproton** (mass ≈ 938 MeV/c², charge = −1e, where e is the elementary charge)
- **1 positron** (mass ≈ 0.511 MeV/c², charge = +1e)

The combined binding energy creates a neutral atom with zero net charge. This neutral nature is crucial: unlike charged antiprotons and positrons, which can be confined in Penning traps using electric and magnetic fields, neutral antihydrogen atoms require different confinement strategies.

### Production Method

At CERN, antihydrogen is produced by mixing antiprotons and positrons:

1. **Antiproton extraction**: The antimatter factory produces antiprotons via 26 GeV proton collisions with iridium targets
2. **Positron sourcing**: Positrons are either produced via pair production or extracted from storage
3. **Sympathetic cooling**: Beryllium ions are added to cool positron clouds via collisional cooling
4. **Mixing**: Cooled positron and antiproton clouds are merged in a Penning trap, forming antihydrogen atoms
5. **Trapping attempt**: Magnetic fields attempt to confine the neutral antihydrogen atoms

### Recent Breakthrough (2025)

The ALPHA collaboration achieved a breakthrough using **sympathetic cooling**:
- **Previous approach**: Direct mixing of positrons and antiprotons was inefficient
- **New technique**: Laser-cooled beryllium ions cool positron clouds to ~−266°C
- **Result**: Production rate increased tenfold; 15,000+ antihydrogen atoms trapped in <7 hours
- **Impact**: Experiments previously taking months now complete in single days

### Confinement Challenges

Confining neutral antihydrogen is extraordinarily difficult:

**Magnetic Bottle Trap**: A non-uniform magnetic field with a minimum at the center creates a potential well for particles with magnetic moments (like antihydrogen). The antihydrogen atom acts like a tiny magnet; it is attracted to the field minimum and trapped.

**Key limitation**: Magnetic confinement is weak—the trap depth is typically only a few Kelvin. Antihydrogen atoms with thermal energy above the trap depth escape and immediately annihilate upon contact with ordinary matter.

**Solution**: Operating at ultra-cold temperatures (~100 mK) ensures most trapped antihydrogen atoms remain confined.

## Current State of the Field

### ALPHA Collaboration Achievements

- **Production**: Record 15,000 antihydrogen atoms trapped simultaneously (2025)
- **Storage**: Individual atoms stored for minutes to hours before loss
- **Precision**: Positional and velocity measurements of confined atoms achieving millimeter-scale precision

### Gravity Measurements (ALPHA-g)

The ALPHA-g experiment measures gravitational acceleration of antihydrogen atoms:

**2023 Result**: First direct measurement showed that antihydrogen falls downward, consistent with a gravitational acceleration of g ≈ 0.75 ± 0.28 × 10 m/s² (consistent with Earth's g ≈ 9.8 m/s²). This ruled out the exotic possibility that antimatter "falls up" (antigravity).

**Precision goal**: GBAR experiment aims for 1% precision gravity measurement using ultracold antihydrogen ions, which would detect even tiny deviations from g = 9.8 m/s².

### Beyond Gravity

- **Spectroscopy**: Measuring the electromagnetic transitions of antihydrogen atoms to test CPT symmetry and fundamental physics
- **Antimatter distribution**: Portable antihydrogen traps enable transport to other laboratories
- **Future experiments**: Proposed measurements of antihydrogen laser spectroscopy, Lamb shift, hyperfine structure

## Open Questions

- **Can antihydrogen atoms be cooled further and stored longer?** Current storage times are minutes; extending this to hours or days would enable new precision measurements
- **Is antihydrogen truly identical to hydrogen?** Spectroscopic measurements test CPT symmetry to increasingly high precision; any deviation would revolutionize physics
- **Can antimatter gravity be measured to 1% precision?** The GBAR experiment pursues this goal; detection of deviations would indicate new physics
- **Can antimatter be synthesized in macroscopic quantities?** Production rates have improved dramatically, but creating gram-scale antimatter remains science fiction
- **Are there applications of antihydrogen beyond fundamental physics?** Medical imaging (PET scans) uses positrons; could antihydrogen enable new diagnostics?
