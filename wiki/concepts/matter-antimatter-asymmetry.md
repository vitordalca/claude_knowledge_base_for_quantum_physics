## Summary

The matter-antimatter asymmetry is one of physics' greatest unsolved mysteries: in the very early universe, the Big Bang created matter and antimatter in nearly equal amounts. Yet almost all antimatter annihilated with matter, leaving only a tiny residual of matter—one in a billion—that now comprises all visible stars, galaxies, and life. This asymmetry has no agreed-upon explanation; the Standard Model's mechanisms can only account for 10⁻¹⁸ of the observed effect, requiring physics beyond current theory.

## Resumo

A assimetria matéria-antimatéria é um dos maiores mistérios não resolvidos da física: no universo primitivo, o Big Bang criou matéria e antimatéria em quantidades quase iguais. Porém, quase toda a antimatéria se aniquilou com a matéria, deixando apenas um resíduo minúsculo—um em um bilhão—que agora compõe todas as estrelas visíveis, galáxias e vida. Esta assimetria não possui explicação aceita; os mecanismos do Modelo Padrão podem explicar apenas 10⁻¹⁸ do efeito observado, exigindo física além da teoria atual.

## Explanation

### The Big Bang Radiation Catastrophe

In the first microseconds after the Big Bang, the universe was extremely hot and dense. Photons had so much energy that they spontaneously converted into particle-antiparticle pairs:

$$\gamma + \gamma \rightarrow e^+ + e^- \quad \text{(or other pairs)}$$

This happened continuously: pairs were created, immediately annihilated back into photons, and created again. The universe was a seething plasma of particles and antiparticles in equilibrium.

As the universe expanded and cooled, photons gradually lost energy. Around 3 seconds after the Big Bang, they became too weak to pair-produce. By this point, every antiparticle should have found its matter counterpart and annihilated, producing only radiation (high-energy photons).

**But we observe a universe filled with matter.** This is the catastrophe: if matter and antimatter were created in equal amounts and annihilated symmetrically, nothing should exist today except photons.

### The One-in-a-Billion Asymmetry

Cosmologists can estimate the asymmetry by observing the **Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)**—the low-energy radiation left over from the early universe's hot phase.

**Key calculation:**
1. The CMB contains ~10⁸⁹ photons in the observable universe
2. Nearly all these photons were created during the first-second annihilation era (matter-antimatter collisions releasing photons)
3. The number of photons per baryon (proton/neutron) is ~10⁹
4. This ratio tells us that for every billion pairs of matter and antimatter that annihilated, **one matter particle survived**

**In other words:** 
- Original matter + antimatter: ~10⁸⁹ each
- After annihilation: ~10⁸⁰ matter particles remain
- Asymmetry ratio: **1 part in a billion**

Every star, planet, animal, and human is made from those surviving "lucky" matter particles.

### The Asymmetry Must Be Fundamental

Two important constraints:

1. **Not due to spatial separation**: In the 1950s, Paul Dirac and Edward Harrison proposed that perhaps antimatter dominates in distant regions (antimatter stars, antigalaxies). But if this were true, boundaries between matter and antimatter regions would release enormous energy from annihilation. Astronomers found no such hotspots in the sky.

2. **True asymmetry, not random fluctuation**: The one-in-a-billion ratio is far too precise and extreme to be explained by random statistical fluctuations. The asymmetry must reflect a **fundamental law of physics**.

## Current State of the Field

### The Standard Model Cannot Explain It

Physicists have known about this asymmetry since the 1960s. In 1973, Makoto Kobayashi and Toshihide Maskawa discovered that the weak force violates **CP symmetry** (charge-parity symmetry)—meaning matter and antimatter behave differently in some interactions.

This was progress, but insufficient:
- The Kobayashi-Maskawa mechanism predicts CP violation strong enough to create an asymmetry of only **10⁻¹⁸**
- We observe an asymmetry of **1 in a billion** (~10⁻⁹)
- The predicted effect is **a billion times too small**

### Constraints from CPT Symmetry

Physics faces a paradox:
- We need asymmetry to explain why we exist
- But asymmetry threatens **CPT symmetry**—the combination of charge, parity, and time-reversal symmetries
- CPT symmetry is built into special relativity and quantum field theory; breaking it would collapse our best theories

The solution: CP violation alone (which breaks CPT) is forbidden. But **T violation combined with P violation** can preserve CPT while creating matter-antimatter asymmetry. This narrowly constrains what new physics is possible.

### Proposed Mechanisms Beyond the Standard Model

**1. Leptogenesis**
- Early universe contains both baryons (matter) and leptons (electrons, neutrinos)
- Some process creates asymmetry in lepton number
- Sphaleron processes convert lepton asymmetry into baryon asymmetry
- Requires neutrino masses and lepton-number violation

**2. Electroweak Baryogenesis**
- The electroweak phase transition in the early universe can produce asymmetry
- Requires CP violation at temperatures ~100 GeV
- Testable via precision measurements of Higgs properties

**3. Grand Unified Theories (GUTs)**
- Unify electromagnetic, weak, and strong forces at very high energies
- Can naturally incorporate CP violation and baryon-number violation
- Predict proton decay; none observed yet (lowers credibility)

**4. Dark Matter + Asymmetry**
- Some models connect dark matter abundance to the baryon asymmetry
- Could explain both mysteries simultaneously

### Active Experiments

**Antimatter Property Tests (CERN)**
- [[entities/alpha-g-experiment]] — Testing whether gravity treats matter and antimatter identically
- [[entities/base-experiment]] — Measuring antiproton magnetic moment to high precision
- [[entities/gbar-experiment]] — Aiming for 1% precision gravity tests

**Particle Accelerator Experiments**
- LHC measurements of CP violation in B mesons and tau leptons
- Rare decay searches (kaons, B mesons) sensitive to new CP-violating interactions

**Cosmological Observations**
- CMB precision measurements (Planck satellite) refining the baryon-to-photon ratio
- Large-scale structure surveys testing matter distribution assumptions

### Recent Developments (2024–2026)

- GBAR experiment at CERN approaching first ultra-cold antihydrogen gravity measurement
- Portable antimatter traps enabling transport of antiprotons (March 2026 test run successful)
- Precision electroweak measurements at LHC tightening constraints on new physics

## Open Questions

- **What is the fundamental cause of the one-in-a-billion asymmetry?** None of the proposed mechanisms in the Standard Model are sufficient.
- **Is it leptogenesis, electroweak baryogenesis, a GUT, or something else entirely?** Each predicts different signatures.
- **Do neutrinos play a role?** Neutrino masses and oscillations might connect to lepton asymmetry.
- **Will GBAR detect asymmetry in gravity?** A positive result would point to new physics.
- **Is there a connection to dark matter?** Could the two greatest cosmic mysteries have a common origin?
- **Are there phase transitions in the early universe we don't yet understand?** Some mechanism might amplify tiny CP-violating effects into the observed asymmetry.
