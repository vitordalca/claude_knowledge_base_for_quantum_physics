## Summary

Chien-Shiung Wu (1912–1997), also known as "Madame Wu," was a Chinese-American experimental physicist who conducted the groundbreaking parity violation experiment in 1957. Her precise measurements of cobalt-60 beta decay showed that the weak nuclear force violates parity symmetry (left-right mirror symmetry), overturning decades of belief in parity conservation. Though the theoretical prediction came from Lee and Yang, Wu designed and executed the experiment. Sadly, the 1957 Nobel Prize went only to Lee and Yang; Wu was excluded, representing a significant oversight in Nobel history. Despite this, she remains celebrated as one of the greatest experimentalists of the 20th century.

## Resumo

Chien-Shiung Wu (1912–1997), também conhecida como "Madame Wu," foi uma física experimental sino-americana que realizou o experimento revolucionário de violação de paridade em 1957. Suas medições precisas do decaimento beta do cobalto-60 mostraram que a força nuclear fraca viola a simetria de paridade (simetria de espelho esquerda-direita), derrubando décadas de crença em conservação de paridade. Embora a previsão teórica viesse de Lee e Yang, Wu desenhou e executou o experimento. Infelizmente, o Prêmio Nobel de 1957 foi concedido apenas a Lee e Yang; Wu foi excluída, representando uma omissão significativa na história do Nobel. Apesar disso, ela permanece celebrada como uma das maiores experimentalistas do século XX.

## Details

### Life and Career

- **Born:** May 29, 1912, in Liuhe, Jiangsu Province, China
- **Education:** University of California, Berkeley (Ph.D. physics, 1940)
- **Career:** Columbia University (1944–1981); later Yale University
- **Awards:** U.S. Presidential Medal of Freedom (1975); numerous honorary doctorates
- **Died:** February 16, 1997, in New York City

### Major Contribution: Parity Violation Experiment (1957)

**Background**
- For decades, physicists believed in **parity conservation** (mirror symmetry): laws of physics should be identical in a universe and its mirror image
- In 1956, theoretical physicists Tsung-Dao Lee and Chen-Ning Yang proposed that parity might be violated in the weak nuclear force
- They asked Chien-Shiung Wu to test this hypothesis

**Experimental Design**
- **Isotope:** Cobalt-60 (radioactive)
- **Process:** Cobalt-60 nuclei decay via beta decay (neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino)
- **Setup:** Align cobalt-60 nuclear spins in strong magnetic field; measure directional preference of emitted electrons
- **Prediction:** If parity is conserved, electrons should be emitted equally in both directions. If violated, one direction preferred.

**Results**
- Wu's measurements showed **~60% of electrons emitted in one preferred direction**
- This violated parity conservation: the mirror image would show opposite handedness
- Physics truly distinguishes left from right at the weak-force level
- *Parity is not conserved.*

**Impact**
- Overturned a fundamental symmetry assumed for decades
- Won 1957 Nobel Prize in Physics for Lee and Yang (theory)
- Wu was not nominated, despite designing and executing the critical experiment
- Jack Steinberger (1988 Nobel laureate) later called this "the biggest mistake in Nobel committee history"

### Scientific Contributions

**Beyond Parity Violation**
- Distinguished experimentalist across nuclear and particle physics
- Measured weak interaction coupling constants with high precision
- Pioneered experimental techniques in beta decay, rare decays, and nuclear reactions
- Over 180 peer-reviewed publications

**Character**
- Known for meticulous experimental design and attention to detail
- Worked intensively: "canceled trips, worked straight over holiday breaks" during parity experiment
- Quiet but determined; let her work speak for itself
- Mentored many students; championed women in science

### Nobel Prize Omission

In 1957, Lee, Yang, and Wu's collaborators expected all three to be honored. Instead, only Lee and Yang won. Theories about why Wu was excluded:
1. **Sexism:** Fewer women recognized by Nobel Committee in physics
2. **Tradition:** Prize often went to theorists over experimentalists
3. **Committee Bias:** Wu's contributions underestimated or overlooked

**Later Recognition**
- Lee and Yang publicly acknowledged Wu's essential role in her speeches
- Multiple historians of science have documented the injustice
- Wu became symbol of gender equity issues in science

### Legacy and Honors

- **U.S. Presidential Medal of Freedom** (1975)
- **China's Jiang Menglin Award** — First Chinese woman to receive
- **Many honorary doctorates** and named lectures
- **Statue at Columbia University** (2018) — first female Chinese physicist so honored

## Impact on Physics and Society

**Scientific Impact:**
- Shattered belief in universal left-right symmetry
- Opened door to CP violation research (discovered 1964)
- Connected weak force to matter-antimatter asymmetry problem

**Social Impact:**
- Pioneered women in experimental physics
- Her exclusion from Nobel Prize became famous example of gender bias
- Inspired generations of women physicists
